# LLM Cost-Optimisation Benchmark v1

*How four production frontier models perform under batch, compression, and output-cap levers — an empirical benchmark of 1,280 scored responses across four task categories, May 2026.*

---

## TL;DR

If you are running LLM workloads in production and want to apply cost optimisations without paying for them in quality, the answer for most task shapes is **gpt-5.4-mini + batch**. That cell delivers canonical quality 0.881 at $0.000557 per task — 94.2% of the frontier quality available across the 16 (model, lever) combinations we tested, at 13.2% of the frontier cost. The frontier itself (gpt-5.4 + baseline at canon 0.935, $0.004220 per task) costs 7.6× more for the remaining 5.4 canonical points.

Across all four models and four task categories, six findings are robust enough to act on:

- **Batch is universally near-free.** ~50% cost cut for ≤2% canonical loss across every model and every task category. On GPT-5.4 specifically, batch produces a bit-for-bit identical Tier-1 pass set vs sync at temperature=0 (54/54 prompts).
- **Compression damages quality universally (−18 to −22 canonical points aggregate), catastrophic on RAG (−45 points, Tier-1 pass rate collapses 80% → 4%).**
- **Output cap is reasoning-specific damage.** Fine on short-answer tasks (≤ −0.07 canonical). On multi-step reasoning, it costs −0.13 canonical because it truncates the answer.
- **Provider dominance is task-shaped, not universal.** OpenAI Pareto-dominates the aggregate frontier (4/4 cells) and wins reasoning + summarisation outright. Anthropic Sonnet wins RAG with canon 0.993 (highest cell anywhere), beating the best non-Sonnet cell by 0.008.
- **Anthropic models degrade more under optimisation pressure.** On reasoning, the Sonnet-vs-GPT-5.4 gap widens from 0.027 at baseline to 0.093 under compression. If you are running Anthropic, avoid compression specifically.
- **The cost-quality sweet spot depends on the task.** Three categories tolerate 9–14× cost spread inside the near-best band; summarisation has only 4 near-best cells in a 2.2× band, its cheapest 30× more expensive than any other category's.

## Why this benchmark exists

Production engineering teams running LLM workloads face a question every public benchmark sidesteps: *which cost optimisations actually preserve quality on real workloads, and which ones quietly destroy it?* Capability benchmarks (MMLU, GPQA, AIME) tell you which model is smartest at the frontier. They do not tell you whether enabling the batch API on your customer-support pipeline will save you 50% of cost at no quality loss — or whether enabling LLM-Lingua-style prompt compression on a RAG workload will silently halve your output quality.

The gap matters because the cost-quality decisions production teams actually make are about *operational levers*, not raw model selection. A team that has already chosen a model is now choosing between sync and batch, between full context and compressed context, between unbounded output and a max-tokens cap. These levers each carry different cost savings and different — often hidden — quality costs that depend on the task shape. The lever choice is where significant engineering effort and inference spend get decided.

This v1 measures four frontier models (claude-haiku-4-5, claude-sonnet-4-6, gpt-5.4, gpt-5.4-mini) under four levers (baseline sync, batch, prompt compression, output cap) across four Tier-2-scored task categories (customer support, RAG QA, reasoning, summarisation), with 20 prompts per category — 80 prompts × 4 models × 4 levers = 1,280 scored responses. Every response was scored on a two-tier rubric (deterministic Tier-1, dual-judge Tier-2 with hybrid human arbitration on disagreements). The output is a 16-cell cost-quality matrix per category and a 64-cell breakdown by model — enough to ground every claim in the findings below in a specific cell.

## Methodology

### Task taxonomy

The benchmark uses 102 prompts across five task categories chosen to cover the four production workload shapes that drive most enterprise inference spend, plus one Tier-1-only category for format-rigour testing:

- **Customer support** (20 prompts): inbox triage with structured JSON output — classify intent, draft a reply. Tests both content accuracy and output-format discipline.
- **RAG QA** (20 prompts): retrieval-grounded short-answer questions with supporting-citation requirements. Tests grounding fidelity and citation correctness.
- **Reasoning** (20 prompts): multi-step quantitative reasoning — proration calculations, conditional logic chains. Tests chained intermediate steps and final-answer correctness.
- **Summarisation** (20 prompts): long-document compression to executive summaries. Free-text output, no deterministic content check applies.
- **Extraction** (22 prompts, Tier-1-only): structured-field extraction from unstructured text. Excluded from Tier-2 judge scoring because the correct answer is exactly verifiable; included only in Tier-1 deterministic pass-rate analysis.

Prompts span easy / medium / hard complexity within each category and were authored to require non-trivial competence — not toy examples.

### Models

Four production-tier frontier models, two per provider, chosen to give a flagship vs cost-tier comparison within each family: **claude-sonnet-4-6** and **claude-haiku-4-5** (Anthropic); **gpt-5.4-2026-03-05** and **gpt-5.4-mini-2026-03-17** (OpenAI). Full model IDs appear in CSVs and methodology; friendly names (sonnet, haiku, gpt-5.4, gpt-5.4-mini) appear throughout the writeup.

### Levers

Four operational levers, each tested on the same 80 prompts × 4 models = 320 cells:

- **Baseline (sync):** standard synchronous API call. Reference cost and reference quality.
- **Batch:** provider batch API, ~24-hour turnaround. Costs 50% of sync at both providers.
- **Compression:** runtime prompt compression via LLM-Lingua at the lever boundary (not preprocessing). Targets 30–50% input-token reduction.
- **Output cap:** explicit max-tokens cap on the response, chosen per-task-category to reflect "what a sensible engineer would set". Tests the trade-off between output budget and quality.

### Two-tier scoring

Every response is scored on two independent tiers, so cost and quality can be evaluated jointly without a single metric collapsing both signals.

**Tier-1 (deterministic):** automated checks per task category — JSON validity, schema conformance, content checks against the expected answer. Produces a status enum (pass, fail_format, fail_schema, fail_content, truncated, compression_unavailable, not_applicable, error). Tier-1 pass rate gives the "is the output structurally usable" signal.

**Tier-2 (judge-based):** LLM judge panel scores each response on a category-specific rubric (0.0–1.0). The v1 panel is a two-judge cross-provider design: claude-opus-4-6 (Judge A) + gpt-5.5 (Judge B). Each judge scores independently against a pre-written rubric. Both judges share families with test models (Opus with Sonnet and Haiku; GPT-5.5 with the GPT-5.4 family); this is a documented v1 limitation discussed in §Limitations.

### Judge panel and panel revision (the credibility section)

The original v1 panel was Opus 4.6 + Mistral large 2512. After Day 10 produced 1,280 Tier-2 scores, an audit of judge behaviour surfaced three specific Mistral failure modes: non-determinism at temperature=0 (score drift across re-fires of the same prompt), score-reasoning desync (numeric score inconsistent with the rendered reasoning text), and — most critically — **hallucinated completeness on truncated responses**. The canonical example is `rea-015 / gpt-5.4 / output_cap`: Mistral claimed "the response correctly arrives at the final answer" and assigned a high score on a reasoning prompt where the response had been truncated mid-March computation by the output_cap lever. Mistral was scoring the answer it expected to see, not the response actually produced. A cross-provider calibration offset (Anthropic family Δ −0.077 vs the eventual GPT-5.5 judge) compounded the case for replacement.

The decision was to replace Mistral mid-benchmark. Three candidate replacements were evaluated against a 12-prompt targeted validation set covering the known Mistral failure modes: **GPT-5.5** (passed 4/4 truncated-output catches, 0 hallucinations, no systematic provider bias — adopted), **Gemini 2.5 Pro** (1/6 truncated-output hallucinations, 4/12 extreme under-scoring on reasoning — rejected as a panel member but retained in code for v2), **Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview** (rejected on operational grounds: ~14 minutes/call under mandatory thinking-mode).

The Mistral data was archived (not deleted) — preserved verbatim in `judge_b_mistral_*` columns of the results table for v2 cross-judge analysis and methodology transparency. All Day 11+ analysis uses the Opus + GPT-5.5 panel. Empirically, the panel revision cut disagreement count from 167 to 80 (13.0% → 6.2%, −52%) and eliminated a Mistral-driven cross-provider calibration offset that had been pulling Anthropic scores systematically low.

### Hybrid arbitration

Of the 80 panel disagreements (cases where |judge_a − judge_b| ≥ 0.3), 16 were arbitrated by a human operator (the prompts with the largest deltas, |Δ| > 0.3). The remaining 64 (|Δ| ≤ 0.3) were resolved by median-canonical-auto — taking the midpoint between the two judges. Both arbitration methods produce a single `canonical_score` per row; the column tags `arbitration_method` so downstream analysis can separate the two populations if needed. Human-arbitrated cases concentrate on prompts where the judges disagreed substantively (one judge thought the answer was right, the other thought it was wrong); auto-median cases are smaller-magnitude calibration noise.

The canonical_score column (one float per Tier-2 row) is the single per-row Tier-2 quality number every finding below is computed against. Agreement-row canon is the mean of the two judges. Disagreement-row canon is the arbitrated value.

## Findings

### Finding 1 — Batch is operationally equivalent to sync (and 50% cheaper)

The empirically strongest version of this finding: on gpt-5.4 specifically, the batch endpoint produces a bit-for-bit identical Tier-1 pass set versus sync at temperature=0 — 54/54 pass intersection on 80 prompts, zero per-prompt divergence. The OpenAI batch endpoint is deterministic-equivalent to sync for this model. Most engineers know batch costs ~50% (the provider price page says so); few have empirical confirmation that batch outputs are operationally equivalent to sync.

Across the broader panel, the cost saving holds at provider-documented rates (0.476–0.501 cost ratios across all four models, matching the 50% batch discount at both Anthropic and OpenAI), and the quality cost is essentially zero: aggregate Δcanonical_score ranges from −0.017 (sonnet) to +0.001 (haiku); per-category Δcanon ranges from 0.000 (customer_support, rag_qa) to −0.015 (reasoning). The three non-GPT-5.4 models show small monotonic regression under batch — 1–2 prompts per model where sync passes Tier-1 but batch fails — and every observed divergence is a reasoning prompt, consistent with reasoning being the noisiest category. None of those regressions move aggregate canonical quality measurably.

The practical implication is unambiguous: if your workload tolerates batch latency, switch. The 50% cost saving comes with no meaningful quality cost on any of the four task shapes we tested. The only reason not to enable batch is latency-sensitivity; the cost-quality decision itself is over.

### Finding 2 — Compression is universally dominated, catastrophic on RAG

Prompt compression cuts cost by only 18–21% while reducing canonical_score by 18–22 points on aggregate (Δcanon −0.180 to −0.224 across the four models — see [Figure 2](../../analysis/out/charts/category_heatmap.png), where the compression column is visibly the worst in every category panel). Cost savings are modest because compression touches input tokens only, not output, and most lever cost on these workloads sits in output. Quality damage is large because compression discards information the model needs.

The category-level damage is wildly uneven. Customer support loses 5 canonical points to compression; summarisation 7; reasoning 21. **Retrieval-grounded QA loses 45 canonical points** — and that aggregate number understates the failure mode. The Tier-1 applicable pass rate on compressed RAG goes from 80.0% (baseline) to 3.8% (compression). Roughly 4 of every 100 compressed RAG outputs pass deterministic format/content checks. Compressing the retrieval context breaks the grounding the model needs to answer correctly; the model produces a structurally plausible but factually wrong answer, which the Tier-1 content check correctly fails.

No model and no task category recovers compression's quality cost. On the cost-quality frontier, the lever is dominated everywhere — for every (model, compression) cell, there is at least one (model, lever) cell with both higher canonical_score *and* lower cost. The conclusion: do not enable prompt compression on RAG workloads on the basis of this benchmark; on other categories, the trade is roughly 1 cost point saved per 1 quality point lost, which is a bad trade.

### Finding 3 — Output cap damage is reasoning-specific

The output-length cap is fine for short-answer tasks (Δcanon −0.004 on customer_support, −0.003 on rag_qa, −0.067 on summarisation) but costly for multi-step reasoning (Δcanon −0.132). The mechanism is visible in the Tier-1 status enum: capped reasoning rows hit the cap (`tier_1_status='truncated'`) at rates of 4–11 per (model, lever) cell on reasoning, versus zero truncations elsewhere except for some long summaries. Capping output cuts the reasoning chain before the answer arrives, and the answer suffers.

The asymmetry is sharp: output_cap costs roughly 4× more quality on reasoning than on the next-worst category (summarisation), and roughly 33× more than on customer_support. This makes the lever a *task-specific* trade, not a general one. Use output_cap freely for classification, extraction, or short replies. Avoid it on tasks that require chained intermediate steps — proration calculations, multi-condition logic, anything where the model needs to show work before answering.

*Footnote: Summarisation is moderately sensitive to both compression (−0.069) and output_cap (−0.067) — the close magnitudes reflect the task's general sensitivity to any quality-affecting lever, not a coincidence.*

### Finding 4 — Provider dominance is task-shaped, not universal

OpenAI models Pareto-dominate the aggregate cost-quality frontier (all four Pareto-optimal cells are gpt-5.4 or gpt-5.4-mini — see [Figure 1](../../analysis/out/charts/cost_quality_scatter.png)), but the per-category Pareto frontiers tell a different story. On reasoning and summarisation, the per-category frontier is OpenAI-only (4 cells each). On customer_support the frontier is OpenAI-only too (2 cells), but the win is so narrow it is effectively a tie — the two OpenAI Pareto cells on customer_support (gpt-5.4 baseline and gpt-5.4 batch) are within 0.007 canonical points of each other — a tie in practical terms.

**On RAG QA the Pareto frontier is mixed, and the highest-canon cell in the entire 16-cell aggregate matrix is sonnet baseline at 0.993 — beating the best non-Sonnet RAG cell (gpt-5.4-mini batch at 0.985) by 0.008 canonical points.** Sonnet batch (0.990) is also Pareto-optimal on RAG, alongside gpt-5.4-mini batch. Sonnet's strength on retrieval-grounded QA outweighs GPT-5.4's strength elsewhere on this category.

The honest framing is: OpenAI wins three of four categories outright; Anthropic Sonnet wins RAG. The "OpenAI Pareto-dominates" headline at the aggregate level is true on average but misses the task-specific structure. A team running mixed workloads should not pick a single provider on this benchmark alone; a team running mostly RAG should look at Sonnet seriously even though the aggregate Pareto frontier excludes it.

### Finding 5 — Anthropic models degrade more under optimisation pressure

At baseline, the canonical-score gap between Sonnet and GPT-5.4 on reasoning is small: 0.943 vs 0.970, a 0.027-point gap that is well within rubric noise. Under compression the gap widens to 0.708 vs 0.801 (0.093 points), and under output_cap to 0.796 vs 0.901 (0.105 points) — see [Figure 3](../../analysis/out/charts/reasoning_drilldown.png), where the compression and output_cap bars in the sonnet and haiku groups are visibly the shortest cells on each model's strip.

The same pattern shows on the cost-tier models. Haiku reasoning canon falls from 0.909 (baseline) to 0.631 (compression) — a 28-point drop — versus gpt-5.4-mini's 17-point drop (0.932 → 0.761) under the same lever. Anthropic models are more sensitive to optimisation levers than OpenAI models on the reasoning category specifically. The pattern is consistent across model tiers and across both compression and output_cap, so it is unlikely to be model-specific noise; it reads as a structural difference in how the two families handle quality under reduced input or output budgets.

The practical implication is asymmetric: if you are running Anthropic models in production, avoid compression specifically on reasoning-heavy workloads — the cost saving is small (≤21%) and the quality cost is disproportionately large. If you are running OpenAI, the same levers cost less. This finding shifts the lever choice from "what does the lever do?" to "what does the lever do *on my chosen provider?*".

### Finding 6 — The cost-quality sweet spot depends on the task

For three of four task categories you can shop aggressively for cheap quality (see [Figure 4](../../analysis/out/charts/cost_sensitivity.png), where the near-best band on each strip spans a wide cost range): customer_support has 9 cells within 90% of the category's best canonical score, spanning a 10.0× cost range (cheapest gpt-5.4-mini batch at $0.000170 per task); rag_qa has 9 cells at 8.9× spread; reasoning has 10 cells at 13.9× spread (cheapest gpt-5.4-mini batch at $0.000431 per task). gpt-5.4-mini batch is the cheapest near-best cell in three of four categories — the cost-quality default for production routing on these task shapes.

Summarisation is the exception. Only 4 cells reach 90% of the category's best canon, all within a narrow 2.2× cost range, and all from the two larger models at baseline or batch. Summarisation's cheapest near-best cell is gpt-5.4 batch at $0.005045 per task — **30× more expensive than the cheapest near-best cell in any other category.** You cannot cheap-route to good summaries on this prompt set; quality requires baseline or batch on a frontier model.

The lesson is task-shaped routing. A production traffic mix that is 80% short-answer + 20% summarisation should route the short-answer share to gpt-5.4-mini batch (~$0.0004 per task) and the summarisation share to gpt-5.4 batch (~$0.005 per task) — a blended cost that is ~85% lower than routing everything to the frontier-quality cell, with the canonical-score loss concentrated on the cheap traffic where it matters least. The cost-quality sweet spot is not a single (model, lever) choice; it is a routing policy.

## The cost-quality sweet spot

Two scalar findings drop out of the analysis cleanly enough to write down as the v1 actionable recommendation:

**Cheapest 94% of frontier quality: gpt-5.4-mini + batch.** Canonical score 0.881 at $0.000557 per task. 94.2% of frontier quality at 13.2% of frontier cost. This is the cheapest cell on the aggregate Pareto frontier. For routine production traffic that tolerates batch latency, it is the dominant cost-quality choice across three of the four task categories tested.

**Frontier quality cost: gpt-5.4 + baseline.** Canonical score 0.935 at $0.004220 per task. The highest canonical score among the 16 aggregate (model, lever) cells. Use when the 5.4-point canonical margin over the near-frontier cell genuinely matters for the application — high-stakes outputs, customer-visible content where the marginal correctness translates to user trust.

The cost premium between the two cells is **7.6× — for 5.4 canonical points.** Put differently: you can buy 94% of frontier quality for 13% of frontier cost by routing routine traffic to gpt-5.4-mini batch, and pay the 7.6× premium only on the share of traffic where the last 5.4 canonical points justify the cost. For a typical production mix this is a 70–85% cost reduction over routing everything to the frontier-quality cell, with the canonical-score loss concentrated where it matters least.

## Limitations

This is a v1 benchmark with deliberate scope limits. Five constraints worth naming before you act on the findings:

**Single-shot scoring, no statistical replication.** Each (prompt, model, lever) cell was scored once, not n=5 with confidence intervals. Where the benchmark shows a Δcanon of 0.020 between two cells, that delta is the difference of two point estimates, not a tested significant difference. The findings above are robust to this limitation because the effect sizes are large (compression −0.45 on RAG cannot be noise) and consistent across cells (batch is near-free on all four models, not just one) — but the smaller deltas, particularly the customer_support 0.007 win-margin tie, should be read as "indistinguishable" rather than as a directional finding.

**Same-family judge bias.** The Tier-2 panel is Opus 4.6 (Anthropic) + GPT-5.5 (OpenAI). Both judges are from the same model families as the test models, which means a judge could in principle favour its own family. We tested for this: the Day 11 panel revision specifically eliminated a Mistral-driven cross-provider calibration offset (Anthropic Δ from −0.077 to +0.024 after switching to GPT-5.5 as Judge B). Residual same-family bias is possible but small; the headline findings (compression catastrophic on RAG; Sonnet wins RAG; batch is near-free) reproduce on both judges individually, so the bias cannot be the load-bearing source.

**102 prompts is a small benchmark relative to real production workloads.** A real customer-support inbox has tens of thousands of unique intent shapes; 20 hand-authored prompts captures the common patterns but cannot reproduce the long-tail. The benchmark's strength is methodological rigour per prompt (two-judge scoring, hybrid arbitration); its weakness is sample size. The findings above are strongest where they reproduce across categories (batch near-free on all four; compression dominated everywhere) and weakest where they rest on a single category (the Sonnet RAG win, for instance, comes from 20 prompts only).

**May 2026 snapshot.** Model prices and capabilities move. The cost ratios in this benchmark reflect the May 2026 pricing tables (cost_usd in the results table is the computed per-call cost at that pricing). New model releases — particularly OpenAI's expected next-generation flagship and Anthropic's Opus 5 — will shift the frontier within months. Findings about lever *effects* (batch is near-free, compression is catastrophic on RAG) should generalise; findings about specific (model, lever) Pareto positions will not. The recommended re-measurement cadence is quarterly.

**v2 plans, scoped.** v2 plans include exploring family-disjoint judges (Grok 4, fine-tuned specialised evaluators), expanded model coverage, additional levers (prompt caching with measured cache-hit rates, structured outputs vs free-text, system-prompt compression), and statistical replication (n=5 with bootstrap CIs). Specific v2 timing depends on Phase 0.5 discovery findings.

## Conclusion + early-adopter offer

We measured 1,280 scored responses across four production frontier models, four operational levers, and four Tier-2 task categories. The findings are: batch is a near-free 50% cost cut on every model and every task; compression destroys RAG specifically and is dominated everywhere else; output_cap is a reasoning-specific tax; OpenAI Pareto-dominates the aggregate frontier but Sonnet wins RAG; Anthropic models degrade more under optimisation pressure on reasoning; and the cost-quality sweet spot depends entirely on the task shape, with summarisation as the exception that breaks every cheap-routing rule.

The actionable v1 recommendation for production routing is: **gpt-5.4-mini + batch for everything that tolerates batch latency**, with task-specific overrides — gpt-5.4 baseline or batch for summarisation, sonnet baseline or batch for RAG-heavy workloads, and explicit avoidance of compression on RAG and output_cap on reasoning. This routing policy delivers 94% of frontier quality at 13% of frontier cost on the average task, with the cost-quality trade concentrated where it has the least impact.
